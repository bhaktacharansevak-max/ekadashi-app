🪔 Ekadashi — Rules & Why

Why we sometimes fast on Dvādaśī, not Ekādaśī

A true Ekadashi must be pure — free of any trace of the day before it, Daśamī. If even a little Daśamī is still lingering in the last hour and a half before sunrise, the great teachers tell us that day is "pierced," and we set it aside and fast the next day instead — which the ordinary calendar calls Dvādaśī. We are not skipping Ekadashi; we are keeping it in its pure form. This is the viddhā rule, given in the Hari-bhakti-vilāsa.

There is also a rarer reason. On eight specially sacred Dvādaśī days, called Mahā-dvādaśī, the scriptures ask us to fast on the Dvādaśī itself, even when the Ekadashi is already pure. So whenever the fast falls on a Dvādaśī, it is for one of these two reasons — a pierced Ekadashi, or a great Dvādaśī.

Two calendars — Smārta and Vaiṣṇava

You may sometimes see two different Ekadashi dates printed. The general practice fasts whenever Ekadashi simply touches sunrise, even with a little Daśamī before it. Vaiṣṇavas — including ISKCON and our Gauḍīya line — keep the stricter, pure rule, so our day is sometimes one day later. If you follow Krishna, follow the Vaiṣṇava date.

Why the date and time change by place

Ekadashi is a phase of the moon, not a fixed square on the ordinary calendar, so it shifts from year to year. And whether a moon-day counts for you depends on your own sunrise — which is why the very same Ekadashi can be today in Bengaluru and tomorrow in Vṛndāvan. It is never an error, only the local sunrise. Always follow the date for your own place.

Breaking the fast — the Pāraṇā

Breaking the fast has its own right time. It is done the next morning, on Dvādaśī, after sunrise — never while it is still Ekadashi, and never at night. We first let the sacred early portion of Dvādaśī, called Hari-vāsara, pass. The best time is the early morning, the first part of the day, before midday, and before the Dvādaśī itself ends. Because every step of this depends on your sunrise, this app works it out for your own location.

When in doubt, follow any local ISKCON temple or Hare Krishna center. Hare Krishna. 🙏
